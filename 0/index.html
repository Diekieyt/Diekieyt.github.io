Becoming Friends with Your Camera

Part 1: Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Figure 1: Without Zoom -- Wrong Way

Figure 2: Zoom in -- Right Way

This is due to the effect of focal length on perspective. The close-up, un-zoomed shot uses a short focal length (a wide angle), which exaggerates the relative size of objects close to the lens. This makes your nose and the center of your face appear disproportionately large, causing distortion. The zoomed-in shot from afar uses a long focal length (a telephoto view), which compresses perspective. By standing farther away, the distances between your facial features become less significant relative to the camera, resulting in a more flattened, true-to-life, and often more flattering portrait.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Figure 3: Without Zoom

Figure 4: Zoom in

Similar to the selfie example, this is an effect of perspective compression. A long focal length narrows the field of view, making background elements appear larger and closer to the foreground than they actually are. This "flattens" the scene and creates the illusion that the path is much more condensed than it appears in the wide-angle shot, which emphasizes depth.

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom

Figure 5: The Dolly Zoom GIF of Silksong!

I placed a Switch console displaying an image from the game Silksong in front of a window (in hopeful anticipation of its release tomorrow!), and then took 7 photos from different distances, zooming in each time to keep the console at a similar size. These were finally combined into this GIF. As you can see, the scenery outside the window continuously enlarges, creating a dramatic push-pull sensation in the image.
